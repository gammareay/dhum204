Influence of Telegraph Communication on Message Form

Nicholas Okken | DHUM 204

With this project, I aim to show the connection between communication methods and the types of content included, specifically focusing on the shift from letters to the early era of the telegram.

Text Comparison: 19th-Century Letters and Telegrams

To compare the contents of various communication forms, I sourced 19th-century letters from the Library of Congress and a variety of early telegram records, including telegrams from the original 1858 Transatlantic Telegraph Cable, the American Civil War, between Emily Vanderbilt Hammond and Martha Berry, and random users of the Western Union Telegraph Company in Ohio.

To visualize this text data, the most frequently occurring words were visualized by Python’s WordCloud tool. Topics were modelled with UMass’s MALLET tool and rendered into tables using Python Rich. For the topic modelling, the corpus was combined, divided into ten subdivisions, and the most frequently occurring topics in each division were recognized by the tool.

Thought Process & Discussion

When beginning this project, I expected for swift communication methods to increase the viability of sending more personal and frequent updates to family. However, of note is the cost of each communication method at the time. Sending a letter, even across the Atlantic, was only a few cents when these letters were written. Meanwhile, sending a telegram initially cost $0.25 per word, even just within the United States. This per-word basis also encouraged shorter messages that lacked long-winded explanations. This hefty price difference likely deterred many working-class individuals from frequently sending messages using these methods.

With this cost differential, it’s not surprising that most telegrams were used for urgent news such as deaths or for political communication, as the transmission speed outweighed the cost of sending in the case of deaths, and political figures would be more likely to have access to the funds required to consistently use the telegram. Ultimately, the telegram was rarely, if ever, used as a casual communication method in the same ways as letters.

In the future, a more thorough project would likely benefit from finding or manually cleaning additional telegram transcripts. Online, I was able to find only a few pre-cleaned transcripts, including those from the initial 1858 transatlantic cable and select telegrams from Ohio. While I did manually convert the Martha Berry telegrams into a readable format, many more image-based telegram records exist online, and transcribing those could offer a much wider repository of data. As my current sources only included a few contexts, additional telegram records would provide credence to the textual analysis.
