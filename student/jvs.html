The Great Gatsby and the harshness of social class.

Julius van Schijndel| DHUM 204

For my final project, I want to look at The Great Gatsby, by F. Scott Fitzgerald, and how it explores social class and why Gatsby is never fully accepted, even after he becomes wealthy. My main question is what Fitzgerald is trying to say about the difference between old money, new money, and the working class. I plan to focus on the different settings in the novel—West Egg, East Egg, New York City, and the Valley of Ashes—and show how each place is connected to a different social class. I also want to follow the major events of the story to show how Gatsby’s dream of becoming part of Daisy’s world slowly falls apart. To do this, I will use Knight Lab TimelineJS to create a timeline of the events that show class differences, and Voyant Tools to examine repeated words and themes related to money, wealth, status, and dreams. By using these three tools together, I hope I can show that Fitzgerald believes social class in America is much harder to escape than Gatsby thinks.

The authors, Wu and Shen, argue that the different places in The Great Gatsby show the differences between social classes. The East Egg represents old money and the people who were born into riches (Tom and Daisy). The West Egg represents new money (Gatsby). The Valley of Ashes represents poverty and more of the ugly side. The authors argue that the differences in these settings show how it is challenging to get the American Dream. They explain that the map is very important and each place has a deeper meaning than just being there.
