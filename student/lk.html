Income, Influence, and Moral Drift

Logan Kleva | DHUM 204

Project Overview

This digital humanities project examines The Wolf of Wall Street as a narrative shaped by income, power, and moral transformation. The project investigates how increasing wealth alters Jordan Belfort’s identity, behavior, language, and ethical boundaries. By combining text analysis with timeline visualization, this project argues that money operates not merely as a resource, but as an accelerant of excess and moral erosion.

Text and Data Source

The primary data source for this project consists of selected passages from Jordan Belfort’s The Wolf of Wall Street. These excerpts focus on moments where money, income, status, and power are explicitly central to Belfort’s narration and decision-making. The passages were selected to capture linguistic, psychological, and ethical changes across his rise, peak wealth, and eventual collapse.

Digital Methods

This project employs two primary digital humanities tools: Voyant Tools for textual analysis and TimelineJS for narrative visualization. Voyant Tools was used to analyze word frequency, trends, and contextual framing of money-related language across selected excerpts from the text. These linguistic patterns informed how each period of Jordan Belfort’s life was interpreted and categorized.

The results of this analysis were organized in a structured spreadsheet compatible with TimelineJS, allowing textual trends to be mapped onto key narrative moments related to income accumulation, excess, and ethical decline.

Interpretation

Viewed alongside the Voyant analysis, the timeline demonstrates how financial language intensifies as Jordan Belfort’s income rises, coinciding with increased entitlement, risk-taking, and ethical erosion. Notably, even after his legal and financial collapse, money remains central to his self-conception, suggesting that wealth reshapes identity rather than simply enabling behavior.
