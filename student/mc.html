Moby Dick Final Project for DHUM

Michael Catanzarite | DHUM 204

My project will explore whether the sea creatures described in Moby-Dick and in traditional maritime folklore were based on real animals or exaggerated over time through storytelling. My central question is: were legendary sea creatures like the Kraken and Leviathan inspired by real ocean life, or are they purely mythical creations shaped by fear and imagination? I plan to examine descriptions of whales and sea monsters in Moby-Dick and compare them with historical sailor accounts and myths. By analyzing these sources, I will argue that many of these creatures were likely based on real animals, such as giant squids or whales, but became exaggerated over time as stories were retold and mythologized.

Brumbach shows how Leviathan originated in early Mesopotamian and Ugaritic mythology in the sea serpent, Lôtān. The creature is then borrowed in the Hebrew Bible, apocrypha, rabbinic literature, and Christian literature. The key point is that Leviathan demonstrates how external ideas were incorporated and adapted. These stories grew the creature larger, stronger, and more menacing. By the rabbinic era Leviathan is a big deal, with uses including the ritual on the end of the sukkah on Shemini Atzeret. This backs up my project that monsters are created in our stories. Just like Moby-Dick, which turns the sperm whale into a demigod, Leviathan was probably a recollection of existing sea creatures. It evolved to be an unruly force of nature. This also happens when current YouTube clips of sharks and squid stir more terror than there really is. Salvador and Tomotani examine the Kraken. They demonstrate it was inspired by sightings of the giant squid (Architeuthis). It reaches 18 meters and is still poorly known. They trace the legend from medieval times to science. The creature's true nature was explained, but the myth remained. The authors conclude the giant squid is part real and part myth. This is my project theme. A real creature is seen fleetingly, and is turned into a gigantic ship-swallowing creature in a story. We see this in Moby-Dick, which recounts whale attacks, such as the Essex. It exaggerates the whale. It's natural for stories to exaggerate, the article explains. This happens on social media today. A brief video can go viral, and cause exaggerated fear, just as old sea monster stories. Fenwick looks at women in The Odyssey and The Lord of the Rings. She looks at Circe, Calypso and Galadriel. These characters help the hero succeed. They are opposite figures like Scylla, Charybdis, and Shelob. The article suggests that Tolkien draws from older mythical ideas. Sea monsters represent chaos, danger, and the unknown. These myths are transmitted and elaborated. In the book Moby Dick, the whale is heavy with the baggage of older sea monsters. It's scary and biblical. This overemphasis is deliberate, Fenwick explains. Writers recycle older myths to create monsters. Social media does the same today. Reactions and videos create and build fear, perpetuating the cycle.
