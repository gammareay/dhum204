Mapping Class and Language in The Great Gatsby

Eric Castner | DHUM 204

This project takes a deep dive into The Great Gatsby by F. Scott Fitzgerald. This will take shape around a guiding question: How do patterns of language and geography in The Great Gatsby work together to reveal and reinforce structures of wealth, class, and social division? Fitzgerald creates a class structure through not only plot and character development, but also by recurring word patterns and spatial organization, and more specifically, physical division, throughout the novel. Time functions as a secondary element that reinforces the same themes. Voyant Tools is used to move deeper into these worlds, past general observations. More specifically, it will provide data of word frequency, character prominence, word associations, and trends over the course of the novel to show how specific words relate to certain characters, themes, and social groups. Google My Maps is also utilized to map out how Fitzgerald's development of space reinforces the theme of class and wealth disparity. Overall, this project provides viewers with a deeper description that contains clear evidence to support a central argument, which ultimately shows how digital humanities tools can reveal deeper meanings throughout literature. Through connecting language, space, and time, it is clear that Fitzgerald constructed a social world to provide commentary on themes that go deeper than the surface of the novel.

Text Analysis (Voyant)

To move beyond general observations, I used Voyant Tools to analyze patterns of language in The Great Gatsby. Voyant allowed me to examine word frequency, character prominence, and word associations across the entire text. One key finding is Gatsby’s repeated use of the word “sport,” which reflects his attempt to construct a persona that aligns with upper-class identity. Additionally, word associations surrounding Gatsby often relate to performance and image, reinforcing the idea that his identity is carefully curated. As Dianne Bechtel argues, “class transcends wealth in America,” meaning that Gatsby’s language reflects his effort to imitate a class he ultimately cannot join. These patterns show that language in the novel is not random, but structured in a way that reinforces class divisions.

Spatial Analysis (Mapping)

n addition to language, geography plays a major role in reinforcing class divisions. Using Google My Maps, I plotted key locations such as East Egg, West Egg, and New York City. West Egg represents “new money,” where Gatsby resides, while East Egg represents “old money,” where Tom and Daisy live. The physical separation between these spaces reflects deeper social divisions. As Wu and Shen explain, “the division of social classes is reflected in the location and division of geographic space” (Wu and Shen). The bay separating East Egg and West Egg creates an “unbridgeable gap,” symbolizing the inability of Gatsby to fully integrate into elite society. This demonstrates that space in the novel is not just a setting, but a structure that reinforces class hierarchy.

Conclusion

This project demonstrates that class in The Great Gatsby is constructed through both language and geography. Patterns in word usage reveal how characters attempt to present themselves, while spatial divisions reinforce social hierarchy. Although Gatsby appears wealthy, both his language and location mark him as separate from the established elite. Digital tools such as Voyant and mapping platforms help reveal these patterns, showing that Fitzgerald embeds class structure not only in the story, but in how the novel is organized.
